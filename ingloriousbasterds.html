REVIEW OF INGLORIOUS BASTERDS (2009)

“I love rumors! Facts can be so misleading, where rumors, true or false, are often revealing.”

Quentin Tarantino’s Inglourious Basterds is a film that opens with a lie — the deliberate misspelling of its own title — and never stops playing with truth, history, and cinematic expectation. It’s not just a revisionist World War II film; it’s a bold, brutal, and often hilarious reimagining of vengeance, propaganda, and storytelling itself. Fueled by Tarantino’s signature dialogue, impeccable tension, and operatic violence, Inglourious Basterds is both a war film and a film about films — and it’s one of the most electrifying entries in his entire career.

Narrative & Structure

Set in Nazi-occupied France, Inglourious Basterds unfolds as a series of interconnected chapters that converge toward one audacious, blood-soaked finale. The film doesn’t follow a single protagonist but rather weaves together several narratives:

  • Shosanna Dreyfus (Mélanie Laurent), a Jewish woman who narrowly escapes the execution of her family and hides in plain sight as a cinema owner in Paris.
  • The Basterds, a ragtag group of Jewish-American soldiers led by the brutal Lt. Aldo Raine (Brad Pitt), who embark on a guerrilla campaign to scalp and slaughter Nazis.
  • Col. Hans Landa (Christoph Waltz), a manipulative and polyglot Nazi officer, known as the “Jew Hunter,” whose intellect and charm conceal monstrous cruelty.

Each chapter is like a short film in itself — slow-burning, dialogue-heavy, and methodically constructed until the tension snaps like a violin string. From the chilling opening on a dairy farm to the fiery climax in a Parisian theater, Tarantino controls time and tone with the precision of a watchmaker and the flair of a showman.

Performances & Characters

Christoph Waltz’s Hans Landa is one of the greatest villains in modern cinema. Fluent in four languages and dangerously intelligent, Landa is both affable and terrifying — the kind of man who will offer you cream for your strudel moments before ordering your execution. Waltz’s performance is hypnotic, oscillating between polite civility and sadistic menace. He won an Oscar for it, and rightly so — his opening scene alone is a masterclass in suspense.
Brad Pitt’s Lt. Aldo Raine is pure pulp. With his Tennessee twang, over-the-top bravado, and complete lack of subtlety, Raine is Tarantino’s cartoonish counterpoint to the calculating Landa. He’s not so much a character as a force of vengeance — gleeful, loud, and unrelenting.
Mélanie Laurent brings grace and emotional depth to Shosanna, one of Tarantino’s strongest female characters. Her arc — from victim to avenger — is the emotional anchor of the film. Her final scene, projected on a burning screen, is both tragic and triumphant.
Diane Kruger as Bridget von Hammersmark, a German actress and double agent, is elegant and sly. Michael Fassbender, in one of his early breakout roles, is sharp and memorable as Lt. Archie Hicox, a British soldier posing as a German in a doomed undercover mission.

Cinematography & Music

Shot by Robert Richardson, the cinematography is elegant and deliberate. Framing is precise. Lighting evokes classic film noir and wartime thrillers. Scenes are often bathed in shadows or harsh whites, underscoring the constant contrast between civility and brutality.
The soundtrack, true to Tarantino’s style, borrows liberally from Ennio Morricone, David Bowie, and other unexpected sources. The anachronistic use of “Cat People (Putting Out Fire)” by Bowie during a scene of preparation is a bold choice — and it works beautifully. The music isn’t used for realism but for emotional resonance, often telling us what the characters can’t say.

Themes & Subtext

Inglourious Basterds is about vengeance, yes — but it’s also about cinema. The final act takes place in a movie theater, where a film becomes a weapon, and reels of nitrate film literally fuel the flames of justice. Shosanna’s revenge is not with a gun, but with an edited reel of Nazi propaganda turned against its makers.
Tarantino questions the ethics of myth-making and violence. Who are the “good guys”? The Basterds are brutal — torturers and executioners. Landa, despite his evil, is rational, even charming. The film doesn’t ask us to root for the moral high ground — it asks us to root for catharsis.
History is not rewritten — it is burned down, shot in the face, and laughed at. This is Tarantino’s fantasy of justice, where cinema corrects the failures of reality.

Cultural Impact & Legacy

Upon release, Inglourious Basterds was a sensation. Critics and audiences praised its audacity, its tension, and its rewriting of history. It earned eight Oscar nominations, including Best Picture, and won for Best Supporting Actor (Waltz).
More importantly, it showed that Tarantino could move beyond the crime genre and still deliver brilliance. It reignited interest in historical revisionism in film and further cemented Tarantino as a filmmaker with a unique voice — one who writes not just scripts, but cinematic literature.

Final Verdict

Inglourious Basterds is a brilliant, brutal, and bizarre masterpiece. It defies genre, redefines history, and celebrates the power of cinema to destroy — and to avenge. Every scene is a high-wire act of tension, wit, and stylistic flair. It’s a film that dares you to look away, even as it rubs your face in blood and celluloid.
Rating: 9.4/10
